    The idea of culture and its meanings… The concept in its different versions

  • The poet and critic T. s. Eliot in the forties of the last century published his book “Notes towards Defining Culture”. In this book, Eliot was interested in making preliminary observations towards defining culture and defining its relationship to religion and politics. However, these observations did not present culture as an essential part in the movement of the advancement of societies and their civilizational progress.

    As for the literary critic Raymond Williams, he traced the complex origins of the word culture, and came to three meanings that he distinguished between. In the eighteenth century, the word came to mean civilization, referring to a general process of intellectual, spiritual and material progress. The fact is that culture was synonymous with the word civilization, and it belonged to the atmosphere spread by the Enlightenment in Europe, which revered secular development.

    Eighty years after Eliot’s book was published, the most famous British Marxist critic Terry Eagleton challenged the development of the book “The Idea of Culture.” The book was published in 2000 by Blackwell Publishing, and it was published in Arabic by the Arab Center for Research and Policy Studies, translated by Thaer Deeb in 2019.

    And if Eliot focused in his book on defining the relationship of culture to religion and politics, Terry Eagleton, despite his Marxism, drew on a wide range of sources, theories and disciplines “to investigate the ways in which culture is known and used to interpret and interact with the material world.”

    Perhaps the foregoing represents the best introduction to entering the book “The Idea of Culture”, as Eagleton, in the first chapter of the book, traces the origins of the word in the historical dictionary of the language. The search for the historical origins of a word requires investigating the effects of the word in the lexicon, tracing its origins and changing its meanings from one meaning to another and from one environment to another. Perhaps the Arab reader finds it difficult to follow the historical origins of the word, because in addition to not being accustomed to this type of research, the existence of an Arabic historical dictionary that traces the origins of a single thing is rare, except for the tireless efforts made by the Doha Historical Dictionary of the Arabic Language, and the Arabic Language Academy in Egypt.

    Meaning of culture:

    What the book tries to show is that culture has two meanings, one of which is narrow and limited anthropological, and another broad and vague aesthetic, and we are stuck at this moment between two concepts of culture, and what we need is to go beyond these two concepts, because the concept of culture as an analytical concept has become very weak in terms of its analytical ability If measured by any of the basic concepts in sociology. Even if culture, according to the definition of E. B. Taylor, in his book, primitive culture is “that complex whole that includes knowledge, arts, morals, law, custom, and other capabilities,” characterized by openness to the point of recklessness, as culture here is everything that is not transmitted by inheritance.

    As others see, culture is the knowledge of the world that enables humans to find appropriate ways to act in specific contexts, and this definition is similar to the definition of practical wisdom in Aristotle, as it is more concerned with knowing the modalities than knowing the causes, i.e. trying to find a guide to dealing with the world that revolves around human, and don’t care how you interact with him.

    On the other hand, when the Marxist literary critic Raymond Williams put in a specific and complete definition of culture in one of his early articles, among the classic definitions of culture, he did not present it in one sense but rather in four distinct meanings of culture, culture as an individual mental nature, and culture as a state of intellectual development which afflicts an entire society, and culture as the aspect that is concerned with the arts, and culture as a total way of life for a group of human beings.

    We note here that the definition of culture before Williams tended to describe it as an intellectual state or an aspect concerned with the arts. With culture and the arts alone would risk alienating the working class from being cultured, which is the last thing Williams wanted. But with the definition he added to culture, the concept could include workers’ institutions, such as trade unions and cooperatives, and the working class could produce what we can consider a cultural product.

    If we extend the line to its integrity, we will find that there are some critics, such as Terry Eagleton and others, who reject the argument of some Western academic cultural circles that assume that everything that is cultural must be Western. However, culture as a good life and fine arts is not exclusive to the West only, nor is it Limited to traditional bourgeois art, the word now extends to more diverse fields.

    In this regard, Eagleton wonders whether the NATO military alliance belongs to Western culture, or whether Dante, Goethe and Stendhal are the ones who should be included in Western culture, and accuses those who assume that culture is Western in nature that they forget that much of this culture is what appeals to the left of the World Bank, indicating That those who call themselves radicals must complain about the function of this culture in relation to the world and the content of this culture, stressing that the culture that they know is the culture that refers to groups with wealth and privilege.

    Literature does not support the monopoly of culture:

    If we assume that all the values of devoted literature approved support the existing political establishment, we will find that Homer was not a liberal humanist, and Shakespeare issued good words about radical equality while Samuel Johnson saluted what happened in his days of armed popular insurrection in the Caribbean, while Flaubert was He despises the middle classes, and we don’t know of Tolstoy having any time for private property.

    Indeed, what matters here is not the works produced by the greatest writers, but the way in which these works are socially interpreted. After all, literary works are presented as evidence of the unity of the human spirit, or of the superiority of the imaginary over the actual. The main thing when we talk about culture is that its values do not represent any specific form of life, but rather the values of humanity. What happens next is entirely accidental. For example, it is true that there is a specific historical culture known as Europe, and the culture was perfectly embodied on this part of the earth. Cosmic and not abstract at the same time.

    At least the West does not appear to be in a position to win the culture wars now. This is because high culture is too exalted and transcendent to be an effective political force, while post-modern culture is often fragile and far from politics. It may be correct to say: Postmodernism is a useful way for the West to give vent to its proud identity, but this is done within a specific geography, because when it reaches the geography of the postcolonial world in the form of vulgar consumerism, it can sign traditional identities and groups. There are forms of crisis that are far from creation and creativity, and when the relationship is like this, between Europe and the post-colonial world, it will feed fundamentalism, which is the last thing that wants open-minded postmodernism. This is what is happening not only in the geography of the south and post-colonial countries, but rather in fundamentalist regions and sectors within the West itself.

    This means that fundamentalism and anti-fundamentalism are not in any way what they appear to be polar opposites, because anti-fundamentalism under postmodern conditions, and in the world of capitalism may end up serving fundamentalism, just as the final victory of capitalism – we mean by this its cultural vision as it reaches farther regions of the earth – it may also prove to be the most dangerous for capitalism itself.

    capitalism and culture:

    Marxist Terry Eagleton did not lose sight of the effects of capitalism and its scourge on culture. In his opinion, transnational capitalism generates isolation and anxiety as well, as it uproots people, destroys their traditional bonds, and creates a chronic crisis in their identity. Therefore, it promotes cultures of solidarity and defense while claiming to be A new cosmopolitan culture, as the greater the avant-garde of the world, the more widespread the cries of heresy and heresy. For every Parisian perfume in Tokyo, we could find a Nazi serial killer in the prime of life. In other words, what happened in our days is not limited to the involvement of the meaning of culture in a violent conflict by describing culture as an identity only. Rather, the matter entered the map of some of the largest political conflicts of our time, including between the north and south of the world. Culture, in its aesthetic and anthropological sense, does not now constitute two sides of an academic conflict, as we used to explain, but it has become a geopolitical axis, as it has come to represent that difference between the West and others. More generally, the difference has become between Western liberal civilization and all the integrative forms that struggle with this civilization: nationalism, localism, identity politics, neo-fascism, and religious fundamentalism. And this is despite the fact that some of these fusional forms are products of that liberal civilization.

    For socialist thought, capitalism was the first truly global mode of production, and this generated some conditions for a positive kind of universalism. For Marx, universalism should have been achieved at the level of individual privacy, since communism, on the contrary, is a relationship between free individuals, who exist in liberal bourgeois society. Socialism viewed this situation as the ideal situation for building a dedicated universalism, not a capitalist universalism, as capitalist universalism had accomplished its work, gathering a crowd of different cultures, and it remains for socialism now to benefit from this matter by building a universal culture on these differences that capitalism made.
